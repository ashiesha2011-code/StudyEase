Chapter 2 — Polynomials
Zeroes of a Polynomial
A zero of a polynomial p(x) is a value k such that p(k) = 0. Geometrically, the zeroes of p(x) are the x-coordinates of the points where the graph y = p(x) intersects the x-axis. A quadratic polynomial has at most 2 zeroes; a cubic has at most 3.
💡 Examiner tip: Always say "x-coordinate of the point where the graph intersects the x-axis" — not just "where it touches the x-axis." That phrasing gets full marks.
Relationship Between Zeroes and Coefficients
For a quadratic polynomial ax² + bx + c with zeroes α and β:
α + β = −b/a     αβ = c/a
For a cubic polynomial ax³ + bx² + cx + d with zeroes α, β, γ:
α+β+γ = −b/a    αβ+βγ+γα = c/a    αβγ = −d/a
⭐ High-value area: These formulas appear in almost every board paper for 2–3 marks. Remember: Sum = −(coeff of x)/(coeff of x²), Product = constant/(coeff of x²).
